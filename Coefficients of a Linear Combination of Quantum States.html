Coefficients of a Linear Combination of Quantum States

A postulate of quantum mechanics claims that given \(n\) states of a system corresponding to wavefunctions \(\psi_1(x)\), \(\psi_2(x) \dots \psi_n(x)\), we can define a superposition of these states as a linear combination of \(\psi_1(x) \dots \psi_n(x)\): $$ \Psi(x) = c_1\psi_1(x) + c_2\psi_2(x) + \dots + c_n\psi_n(x) $$ where \(c_n\) are complex coefficients.

This article will show that if the quantum states of the system are orthogonal, \(|c_n|^2\) gives the probability of finding the system in the state \(\psi_n(x)\).

Orthogonal States If \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) satisfy \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi_1^*\psi_2 \ dx=0\), these states are said to be orthogonal to each other. This is rather similar to the dot product for vectors — if \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0\), \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are orthogonal to each other, i.e. they do not 'lie' in each other.

For simplicity I'll take \(\Psi=c_1\psi_1+c_2\psi_2\) , a superposition of only two states. The probability density function for this state is thus: $$ \begin{align} |\Psi|^2 &=|c_1\psi_1+c_2\psi_2|^2 \\ &= (c_1\psi_1+c_2\psi_2)(c_1^*\psi_1^*+c_2^*\psi_2^*) \\ &= |c_1|^2|\psi_1|^2+|c_2|^2|\psi_2|^2+c_1\psi_1 c_2^*\psi_2^*+c_1^*\psi_1^* c_2\psi_2 \end{align} $$ The last two terms are interference terms from the interference of the two wavefunctions. It gives a correction to the classical addition of probabilities by taking into account the interference between the wavefunctions.

Given that \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) are orthogonal and \(\Psi\), \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) are normalised, $$ \begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\Psi|^2 \ dx &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|c_1|^2 |\psi_1|^2 \ dx + \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|c_2|^2 |\psi_2|^2 \ dx + c_1 c_2^*\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi_2^* \psi_1 \ dx + c_1^* c_2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\psi_1^* \psi_2 \ dx \\ &= |c_1|^2 + |c_2|^2 \\ &= 1 \end{align} $$ Thus \(|c_1|^2\) and \(|c_2|^2\) are the probabilities of finding \(\Psi\) in the state \(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\) respectively.
