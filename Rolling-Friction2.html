Rolling Friction

Problem

A sperical object is rolling without slipping across a plane. A friction force acts at the point of contact. Does the friction force do work on the object?

A friend was confused and thought the friction force had to do work because \(\int \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}_{cm} \), where \(d\vec{s}_{cm}\) is an infinitesimal path of the centre of mass. Explanation 1 was the explanation I gave (or tried to give). Explanation 2 was the explanation he gave. What do you think?

Explanation 1

Consider an extended rigid object. The work done \(W_i\) on a particle of mass \(m_i\) by a net force \(\vec{F}_i\) acting on it is \(W_i=\int \vec{F}_i\cdot d\vec{s}_i\). The total work done on the object is then $$ W_{tot}=\sum \int \vec{F}_i \cdot d\vec{s}_i \tag{1} $$ For the case of the rolling wheel, the particle on which the friction force acts is instantaneously stationary, hence the work done by the friction force is zero.

Now there is confusion in this case that when thinking about the translational motion of an object, we could consider the work done by a force as \(\int \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}_{cm}\). Going back to equation \((1)\), $$ \begin{align} W_{tot}&=\int \vec{F}_1\cdot d\vec{s}_1+\int \vec{F}_2\cdot d\vec{s}_2+\cdots \\ &=\int \vec{F}_1\cdot d\vec{s}_1+\vec{F}_2\cdot d\vec{s}_2+\cdots \\ &=\int (\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2+\cdots)\cdot d\vec{s}_{cm} \tag{*} \\ &=\int \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}_{cm} \end{align} $$ Step \((*)\) was only possible if all of the particles translated along the same path, i.e. along \(\vec{s}_{cm}\). Hence if the friction force is the net force acting on the wheel, \(W_{tot}=0\).

Now you may argue that since the friction force is a net force, it would cause the wheel to accelerate, hence it should do work on it, because \(\vec{F}=M\vec{a}_{cm}\) right?

Lets take a look at the derivation of the formula.

The net force acting on some particle of mass \(m_i\) in an object is \(\vec{F}_i=m_i\vec{a}_i\). The net force acting on the whole body is thus: $$ \vec{F}=\sum \vec{F}_i=\sum m_i\vec{a}_i $$ We know that $$ \begin{align} \vec{r}_{cm}=\frac{\sum m_i\vec{r}_i}{M} &\implies \vec{v}_{cm}=\frac{\sum m_i\vec{v}_i}{M} \\ &\implies \vec{a}_{cm}=\frac{\sum m_i\vec{a}_i}{M} \end{align} $$ Therefore \(\vec{F}=M \vec{a}_{cm}\).

Note that from the beginning of the derivation, we assumed that the particle's motion depended on the force only (\(\vec{F}_i=m_i\vec{a}_i)\), i.e. there were no constraints on the motion of the body. But in the case of the rolling wheel, there is a constraint and it is that the wheel rolls, hence the formula cannot be applied here.


Explanation 2

$$ KE=\underbrace{\frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2}_{\text{from}\int \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2}_{\text{from}\int \vec{\tau}\cdot d\theta} $$ The friction force reduces the first term but increases the second, hence the net work on the object is zero.

Thoughts

I don't feel quite certain about Explanation 2 because the friction force only acts at an instant at the point of contact, and in that instant the point neither displaces a distance \(ds\) nor rotates through an angle \(d\theta\), hence the work done is trivially zero.

If we think about the object rolling down a plane, the same argument applies. It is the gravitational force doing work on the object, causing it to accelerate down along the plane, increasing \(v_{cm}\) and since \(\omega=v_{cm}/r\), it increases \(\omega\) too. This then leads to the thought that it does not necessarily have to be a torque that contributes to the \(\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2\) term and likewise, a translational force that contributes to the \(\frac{1}{2}mv_{cm}^2\) term. Is it true?
