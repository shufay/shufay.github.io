The Even Potential Theorem

Theorem: If \(V(x)\) is an even function \((V(-x) = V(x))\), then \(\psi(x)\) can always be taken to be either even or odd.

Proof

$$ -\frac{\hbar}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x) = E\psi \label{1} \tag{1} $$ If \(\psi{(x)}\) satisfies \((\ref{1})\), then, changing variables \(x \to -x\) and noting that \(\frac{d^2}{d(-x)^2} = \frac{d^2}{dx^2}\), $$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi(-x)}{dx^2} + V(-x)\psi(-x) = E\psi(-x) $$ so if \(V(-x) = V(x)\), then \(\psi(-x)\) also satisfies \((\ref{1})\). It follows that $$ \psi_{+}(x) = \psi(x)+\psi(-x) $$ which is even: \(\psi_{+}(-x) = \psi_{+}(x)\), and $$ \psi_{-}(x) = \psi(x)-\psi(-x) $$ which is odd: \(\psi_{-}(-x) = -\psi_{-}(x)\), both satisfy \((\ref{1})\). But \(\psi(x) = \frac{1}{2} [\psi_{+}(x)+\psi_{-}(x)]\), so any solution can be expressed as a linear combination of even and odd solutions.
